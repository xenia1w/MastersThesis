\section*{Abstract}
\label{Abstract}

Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) systems have improved significantly in recent years, yet they still struggle with non-native speakers due to prosodic variations and uncommon word usage. This thesis investigates whether ASR models can be self-adapted to individual speakers to improve transcription accuracy. For prosodic abnormalities, speaker-specific features will be extracted using self-supervised speech representations (WavLM) and integrated into a trainable ASR encoder via parameter-efficient adaptation techniques such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA). Lexical adaptation for unusual or user-specific words will be explored using LLMs to generate personalized vocabulary lists for decoder bias. Performance will be evaluated on accented and lexically diverse speech datasets using WER. The study aims to investigate how quickly adaptation becomes effective.


\section{Introduction}
\label{Introduction}

Within the last couple of years Large Language Models (LLMs) have experienced rapid technological advances. The latest models offer help with a variety of actions such as code generation, mathematical tasks execution and agent tasks \cite{Yang2025Qwen3TR}, and at the same time their natural language processing (NLP) skills mimic closely those of humans \cite{Achiam2023GPT4TR}.

The newest technological advances allow the creation of multi-modal large language models (MLLMs), which are capable of listening, speaking, seeing and creating content \cite{Achiam2023GPT4TR, shi2026qwen3, Yang2025Qwen3TR}. %TODO: maybe add differnt references? more references?

Alongside these advances also the Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) systems have improved significantly. Thanks to advanced algorithms and machine learning techniques the ASR systems are well-equipped to transform audio input into text output \cite{oye2025accent}.

As English has established itself as the international language it also resulted in a wide group of non-English speaking users, whose accents make it harder for such ASR systems to correctly annotate their input \cite{jain2025real, song2025mpsa}, which in return leads to expected frustration and anointment from the users \cite{oye2025accent}.


\subsection{Proposed Approach}

To investigate accent-aware ASR, this thesis will explore parameter-efficient adaptation methods that enable models to adjust to speaker-specific prosodic characteristics. In particular, self-supervised speech representations will be used to capture prosodic features, and techniques such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) or adapter-based fine-tuning will be employed to personalize pretrained ASR models.

In addition to prosodic adaptation, the thesis will investigate lexical and stylistic self-adaptation aimed at improving recognition of uncommon, domain-specific, or user-preferred word usage. This will be approached at the decoding level. Transcriptions from earlier audios of the same speaker will be analyzed using an LLM to retrieve recurring vocabulary, domain context, and stylistic patterns. The resulting speaker-specific profile will then be used to condition ASR decoding via prompt-based mechanism.

\subsection{Research Questions}

For the scope of the thesis possible research questions have been formulated: \\

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} Can an ASR be self-adapted to a specific user to significantly improve the accuracy (WER) in the case of prosodic abnormalities?

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{RQ1.1:} What approach is suitable to capture prosodic abnormalities?

        \item \textbf{RQ1.2:} What approach is suitable to give this context to an ASR?

        \item \textbf{RQ1.3:} How long does it take to get significant? (e.g. after how many sentences/words/seconds of audio does it get significant?)
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{RQ2:} Can an ASR be self-adapted to a specific user to significantly improve the accuracy (WER) in the case of textual abnormalities (like uncommon word usage)?

    \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ2.1:} Can an LLM infer stable lexical and stylistic patterns from previously decoded ASR transcripts?

    \item \textbf{RQ2.2:} Does conditioning ASR decoding on LLM-generated prompts improve recognition of uncommon or user-specific word usage?

    \item \textbf{RQ2.3:} How much user speech is required before prompt-based adaptation becomes effective?
\end{itemize}

\end{itemize}


\section{State of Art Research}
\label{StateOfArt}

The importance of the ASR has been recognized by numerous publications, but at the same time its  imperfections have been tried to be addressed by many authors. Anh et al. \cite{anh2024improving} improved the speech recognition with prompt-based contextualized ASR and LLM-based re-predictor. The authors recognized the advantages of the key LLM-component: prompt \cite{reynolds2021prompt}, and decided to leverage it alongside the LLMs to boost the ASR accuracy. They did so by adding a text encoder to the ASR system. Additionally, they sent to the LLMs the n-best hypotheses with corresponding acoustic information and contextualized prompts in order to receive a re-prediction of the ASR script. Their methods proved to be highly effective compared to the baseline methods on different public datasets.

Yu et al. \cite{yu2024connecting} performed analysis on various datasets for the speech encoders from Whisper and LLMs from Vicuna models, where they demonstrated that the LLMs with Q-Formers have a reduced WER over LLMs with other connector structures.

Oye et al. \cite{oye2025accent} created an accent and dialect adaptation in the ASR. The authors trained custom Vosk models to improve the ASR for the non-native English users. They gathered an impressive dataset with a huge variety of accents by combining the publicly available datasets, academic collaborations and crowdsourced recordings. They also argumented the choice of the Vosk models, due to their good latency and throughput. Their work also shows a better performance compared to the baseline approaches.

When concentrating on the accent adaptation for the ASR, also several other solution have been proposed such as \textit{AccentFed} \cite{jain2025real}, which is ethical and real-time adaptable and is well-suited for globally inclusive ASR services. It has also been acknowledged that using a mutlilingual pretraining \cite{javed2022towards} as well as diverse datasets \cite{likhomanenko2020rethinking} leads to better and more robust ASR solutions.

\section{Methods}
\label{Methods}

The goal of this work will be to study whether an ASR system can adapt itself to a specific user in order to improve the transcription accuracy when the speaker displays prosodic abnormalities such as foreign accent, atypical pausing etc. The proposed approach will focus on lightweight speaker adaptation that will require only a small amount of user data and will not rely on full model retraining.

The method will consist of two main components:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item extracting a feature representation of a speaker's prosodic characteristics from snippets of their speech
    \item using the representation to adapt an ASR model during transcription.
\end{enumerate}

Performance will be evaluated using Word Error Rate (WER) on multiple accented speech datasets such as \textit{Speech Accent Archive}\footnote{\url{https://accent.gmu.edu}} and \textit{L2-ARCTIC}\footnote{\url{https://psi.engr.tamu.edu/l2-arctic-corpus/}}.

With these methods I am hoping to answer the questions \textbf{RQ1, RQ1.1, RQ1.2} and \textbf{RQ1.3}.

To tackle the question \textbf{RQ2, RQ2.1, RQ2.2} and \textbf{RQ2.3} I am planning to leverage an LLM to create a summary of the speaker's recurring vocabulary, domain, stylistic traits etc. and later use the ASR's model \textit{prompt} parameter to influence the contextual decoding. The LLM will be fed with increasing snippets of speakers dialogue in order to update the speaker profile periodically.

\subsection{Prosodic Feature Extraction}

Even though the prosody does not necessarily influence the meaning of the words \cite{peppe2009prosody}, it can give a useful insight about the characteristics of the speaker, such as pitch, length, loudness, stress and accents.

To capture the speaker-specific prosodic characteristics, a pretrained WavLM \cite{chen2022wavlm} model will be used. Because WavLM is a self-supervised speech representation model that encodes information about pronunciation, timing, rhythm and other speaker traits directly from the raw audio, it is perfect for encoding the prosodic features of interests. It will be used in frozen manner, so that its parameters will not be updated during the experiments. This approach will provide an answer to \textbf{RQ1.1}.

Speech audio will be resampled to 16 kHz and processed by the WavLM encoder. This will produce frame-level embeddings for each sample. These embeddings will then be summarized into a fixed-size vector by computing simple statistics over time (mean and standard deviation). This will result in a compact prosodic embedding that will represent how a speaker typically pronounces and times their speech.

To enable gradual self-adaptation, embeddings from multiple utterances of the same speaker will be combined using a running average. As more speech from the user will become available, this speaker representation will become increasingly stable and descriptive. With this approach I am hoping to answer \textbf{RQ1.3}.

\subsection{ASR Model and Speaker Adaptation}

For speech recognition, a wav2vec 2.0–based ASR model \cite{baevski2020wav2vec}  will be used. This model is chosen because it provides a trainable acoustic encoder while remaining relatively simple, as compared to the other competitive models: OpenAI Whisper\footnote{\url{https://openai.com/index/whisper/}, Accessed: 30.01.2026} and the Qwen ASR Models \cite{shi2026qwen3}. Unfortunately, both Whisper and Qwen models are blackbox-only approaches and do not directly offer possibilities to inject extra information in the layers. They might be used as baseline approaches.

Speaker adaptation will be implemented using parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques. In particular, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) \cite{hu2022lora} is considered to enable speaker-specific adaptation of the ASR encoder while keeping the base model parameters frozen. This will allow the model to adapt to prosodic characteristics such as accent and speaking rhythm using only a small amount of speaker data.

With this approach I am hoping to provide a direct answer to \textbf{RQ1.2}.

\subsection{LLM-Based Lexical and Stylistic Prompting}
\label{subsec:llm_prompting}

Because LLM prompting \cite{reynolds2021prompt} has emerged as a powerful mechanism for conditioning language generation, this thesis proposes an approach for lexical and stylistic self-adaptation of ASR systems via contextual decoding. Using a suitable speech dataset containing multiple utterances per speaker, several snippets from the same speaker will first be transcribed using an ASR model, such as Whisper or Qwen. The proposed datasets would be \textit{TED-LIUM}\footnote{\url{https://service.tib.eu/ldmservice/dataset/ted-lium-3}}, which contains diverse topics with varied vocabulary and many entries are available with the transcriptions, \textit{AMI Meeting Corpus}\footnote{\url{https://groups.inf.ed.ac.uk/ami/corpus/}}, which offer natural conversations recorded during meetings and often containing the annotations or the \textit{Common Voice (Mozilla)}\footnote{\url{https://datacollective.mozillafoundation.org}}, which offers a variety of many different datasets.

The resulting outputs will be provided to an LLM with the following prompt instruction to extract the speaker's preferred phrasing, domain keywords, name entities etc:

\begin{quote}
    “Summarize the speaker’s recurring vocabulary, domain, register, and stylistic traits relevant for speech transcription, in case there are any. Your answer should be maximally 1-2 sentences long.”
\end{quote}

This should directly provide the answer to the \textbf{RQ2.1}.

Afterwards, the LLM's output will be used as a prompt input to Whisper's and/or Qwen's parameter \textit{prompt}. Using the comparison between the resulting ASR output and the baseline approach and comparison of equivalent WER should give a direct answer to the \textbf{RQ2.2}.

The final step would be to update the speaker language profile periodically as more utterances from the same speaker will become available. The impact of prompt-based adaptation will be evaluated by comparing WER between a baseline ASR system without prompting and the proposed LLM-assisted prompting approach. This analysis will address \textbf{RQ2.3} by examining how much user data is required before prompting yields measurable improvements.
